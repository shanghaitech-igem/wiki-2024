From Gene to Music

DNA sequence visualization has a relatively long history in bioinformatics.

Even the representation of the four bases as letters - A, C, G, and T - and the sequence of a single strand of the double helix as a string of those letters is an easy way to visualize and has a long tradition in databases and publications dealing with DNA sequences.

Visualization of genome annotations, paired sequence alignment maps, and multiple alignments extend visualization further by adding visualizations of sequence properties (e.g., predicted gene structure or similarity to other sequences).

However, like any representation, visualization has its limitations.

In the research context, visualization of a large number of sequences is limited by the size of the computer screen, the ability to convey diverse information through color or symbols, and the attention of the researcher.

In the context of public engagement in science, DNA sequences have a drab visual appearance that is at odds with the â€œdazzlingâ€ usually expected in such public displays or performances.

While the number and seemingly random nature of a large number of DNA sequences is attractive for a short period of time, the level of concentration required for more detailed examination of DNA sequences or annotations is often very high for the public to participate in AD hoc or short-term events.

Therefore, in public activities, on the whole, bioinformatics is relatively lacking in public participation activities.

We aim to help fill this gap through the sonification of DNA, in which the representation of the sequence is auditory rather than visual.

Since music is widely understood and used for relaxation and entertainment, this may be more appealing to a public audience than a screen full of DNA sequences or associated data.

The Logic

By finding and replacing, the bases A, C, G, and T are converted to the A4 note or note 69 in the Music Standard Interface (MIDI), the C4 note (MIDI 60), the G4 note (MIDI 67), and the G#0 note (MIDI 20, chosen because T is the 20th letter of the alphabet), respectively. And use the default synthesizer. A filtered bass drum is added at the beginning of each codon (i.e., every three bases)

The Method

First, you need to download the music software sonic pi with the zip package.

After that, edit the sequence of bases you want to musicalize into a file similar to gulo.fa, open the file dnason with VScode, and type python dnason.py gulo.fa 0 > Sono.txt in terminal (python: environment variable; dnason.py: the executed program, gulo.fa: contains your base sequence) and run it, you get the sonic.txt folder.

Finally, open sonic pi, open the Sonic.txt folder in the software, click Run, and you can hear the melody of genes.

The method of operations from gene to music

From Music to The Base

And then we started thinking, can we match music to genes? In the process of brainstorming, we believe that it is not practical and popular science significance to convert music into base sequences only through some mapping relationship, because the results usually do not correspond to a real gene sequence in reality.

Therefore, we decided to match music with the base sequence of an existing gene through a specific mapping relationship, and to popularize the relevant information about that gene.

The Logic

Convert all C1-7 and D1-7 to base C (cytosine), all E1-7 to base T (thymine), all F1-7 and G1-7 to base G (guanine), and all A1-7 and B1-7 to base A (adenine), and compare the resulting base sequences with our gene pool.

Game1:THE ROAD OF TRANSCRIPTION (By design team members of iGEM ShanghaiTech-China)

â€œThe Road of Transcriptionâ€ is an educational card game designed to help players learn and understand the fundamental processes involved in DNA transcription. It focuses on biological principles such as DNA, RNA, base pairing, and genetic editing tools like CRISPR-Cas9 and TALEN.

Game-play Overview

  • Objective: The game aims to successfully transcribe a sequence by pairing the correct DNA and RNA bases, simulating the biological transcription process.

    The first player to complete ten base pairs wins.

  • Players: At the beginning of the game, each player is dealt ten cards, and the remaining cards form the draw pile.

    Players take turns according to a randomly chosen sequence. They draw four cards on each playerâ€™s turn unless a particular tool card modifies this number.

Physical card display

Card Types

  1. Basic Cards

    • The basic cards represent the four nucleotides (A, T, C, G) in

    • DNA and (A, U, C, G) in RNA.

    • Players place DNA cards (A, T, C, G) into the DNA path and RNA cards (A, U, C, G) into the mRNA path, following the base-pairing rules (A with T or U, C with G).

  2. Function Cards

    • These include synthesis cards, which allow players to draw additional cards and simulate the process of nucleobase synthesis within a cell.

  3. Tool Cards

    • CRISPER-Cas9 and TALEN are two powerful tool cards based on real-life gene-editing technologies. These cards can break sequences on an opponentâ€™s path if the base pairs match the sequence on the tool card.

    • For CRISPR-Cas9, the tool only works if the opponentâ€™s path contains a sequence matching the base pair pattern on the card.

    • TALEN cards work similarly but require an exact match of two specific bases on the path.

  4. Special Function Cards

    • Mutation Cards: These cards introduce genetic mutations. For instance, a base substitution mutation card changes one of the bases on the opponentâ€™s DNA path. The player draws a new card to replace the affected base.

    • Frame Shift Mutation: This card forces an opponent to remove base pairs until their path contains a multiple of three base pairs, simulating the effect of frame-shift mutations in genetic coding.

    • Transcription Activators: These unique cards allow players to substitute any base with a transcription factor, speeding up the transcription process.

Educational Value

The game has a strong foundation in biological education, simulating the real-life DNA transcription process. Players learn about the complementary base-pairing rules between DNA and RNA and the role of enzymes and proteins involved in gene editing and transcription. Each card is designed to represent actual biological functions and processes, though simplified for game-play purposes.

For example:

  • Base Pairing: Players must adhere to strict rules, placing adenine (A) with thymine (T) or uracil (U) and cytosine (C) with guanine (G), just like in biological systems.

  • Gene Editing: CRISPR-Cas9 and TALEN cards represent advanced biotechnologies used in gene editing, allowing players to â€œeditâ€ their opponentâ€™s genetic sequences.

Strategy

The gameâ€™s strategy lies in how players use their function and tool cards. Players can sabotage their opponentsâ€™ paths using mutation or gene-editing tools, forcing them to correct errors before proceeding with transcription. Properly utilizing these function cards can slow opponents down or prevent them from winning if used wisely.

â€œThe Road of Transcriptionâ€ offers biological education and strategic game-play. It helps players understand the transcription process and related biological concepts while engaging in a competitive and fun environment. Players can gain a deeper appreciation for the complexity and precision of genetic processes through interaction with concepts like base pairing, gene editing, and mutation.

Game2:VOYAGE OF BACTERIA(By Yiping Zeng and Di Tian)

Overview

â€œVoyage of Bacteriaâ€ is a strategy-based board game that uniquely incorporates rigorous knowledge of synthetic biology. Primarily drawing upon concepts typically introduced at the high school level, the game builds on this foundation by expanding the biological scope, offering a fun yet educational experience.

Players can immerse themselves in a thrilling competition while gaining insights into genetic engineering, enzyme functions, and microbial growth. Even when a playerâ€™s bacteria are eliminated, they are not out of the game, as they can join other research teams and continue playing. Every player is engaged throughout the entire game, making it an enjoyable and interactive experience from start to finish.

Game Setting

Players step into the shoes of brilliant synthetic biologists engaged in friendly but fierce bacterial competition. The objective is to manipulate bacteria through genetic engineering, making them more robust, more resistant, and ultimately victorious over other playersâ€™ bacterial strains in a race to dominate the petri dish.

Players must carefully strategize, using genes, enzymes, and tools to alter their bacteria, enhance combat abilities, and expand across the Petri dishâ€™s territory. With each turn, they get closer to either domination or elimination, but teamwork offers them the chance for redemption.

Game Features

The game features several distinct elements that make it stand out, combining fun with learning:

  1. Synthetic Biology Integration

While the gameâ€™s mechanics are easy to grasp, it embeds authentic synthetic biology concepts. For example, players must use restriction enzymes and ligases to modify bacterial genomes realistically, offering a deep dive into genetic manipulation.

It serves as a subtle educational tool that helps spread synthetic biology knowledge.

  1. Survival Beyond Defeat

Even if a playerâ€™s bacterial strain is eradicated, they can still join other teams, ensuring continuous engagement.

By acting as a secondary researcher for different teams, players are never out of the game, maintaining excitement throughout the session.

  1. Territorial Expansion

The gameâ€™s petri dish setting is divided into hexagonal cells, and players must strategically grow their bacterial colonies across these spaces.

Combat is initiated when bacterial colonies from different teams come into contact, requiring careful planning and using cards to attack or defend.

Physical card display

Game-play Mechanics

At the start of the game, each player selects a bacterial strain from a deck of â€œCharacter Cards.â€ These strains include E. coli, Diatoms, Shewanella, and more exotic organisms like Greenlandic Prehistoric Bacteria.

Players then position their bacteria in one of the hexagonal spaces within the petri dish. With each turn, players draw cards from the deck and perform one of two possible actions: use a card to enhance their bacteria or launch an attack on an adjacent bacterial colony.

Bacteria grow and replicate every few turns, varying growth rates by species.

Players gain attack or defense abilities When they successfully modify their bacteria with new genes, such as toxin-producing genes or antibiotic-resistance genes.

Players must carefully balance growth, defense, and attack to outwit their opponents.

Once the bacteria come into contact, they can engage in battle, which results in either the destruction of some of the opponentâ€™s bacteria or the withdrawal of bacteria from a contested area.

Cards and Strategic Elements

  • Character Cards (Bacterial Strains): Each strain has unique traits and corresponding expression vectors that guide genetic engineering possibilities.

    For example, E. coli is a versatile strain with no special abilities but multiplies. At the same time, Diatoms have a silica-covered cell wall that offers them a natural defense against attacks.

  • Item Cards (Enzymes): These cards represent enzymes such as T4 DNA ligase, E. coli DNA ligase, and restriction enzymes. Each enzyme allows players to perform specific genetic modifications, giving their bacteria new strengths.

    For instance, restriction enzymes are necessary for cutting out genes from plasmids. At the same time, ligases connect genes to bacterial genomes.

  • Gene Cards: These cards feature genes that provide either attack or defense capabilities.

    For example, the aflatoxin gene boosts a bacterial strainâ€™s attack by 5 points. In comparison, the tetracycline resistance gene boosts defense by 3 points. The game requires players to intelligently combine these genes to create a strain that can survive and conquer.

  • Environment Cards: These cards offer environmental modifications or tools that can change the course of the game.

    Some cards may double the gene expression in bacteria, while others provide the necessary enzymes for gene editing.

  • Attack and Defense Mechanisms: A battle ensues when two opposing bacteria colonies meet.

    There are specific requirements for initiating an attack:

    1. Bacteria must be adjacent to the petri dish.
    2. The attacking bacteria must use the appropriate genes or cards to launch the attack. After an attack, the targeted bacteria in adjacent hexagonal cells are killed, leaving the cells unoccupied.

    However, bacteria are not entirely wiped out in one attack; only a specific number of cells are cleared based on the attack power. The gameâ€™s strategic depth comes from deciding when to attack and when to defend, and players must be mindful of gene combinations to strengthen their bacterial colonies.

  • Regeneration and Switching Teams: One of the gameâ€™s unique features is the ability to regenerate or switch teams even after total bacterial destruction.

    For example, the â€œGreenlandic Prehistoric Bacteriaâ€ can regenerate in any part of the petri dish and destroy surrounding bacteria upon revival, offering a second chance to dominate the game.

    Alternatively, when a playerâ€™s bacteria are destroyed, they can join another research team as an assistant, contributing their previously engineered expression vectors to the new teamâ€™s efforts.

  • Bacteria Enhancement Steps: To enhance bacterial abilities, players follow a series of genetic engineering steps:

    1. Extract the target gene from a plasmid using the appropriate restriction enzymes.
    2. Use a second set of restriction enzymes to open the bacterial expression vector.
    3. Ligate the gene into the bacterial genome using DNA ligases, with sticky or blunt ends requiring different approaches.

    During this process, opponents can use item cards to disrupt your progress. However, once the ligation step is complete, interference is no longer possible, and the bacteria gain their new ability.

  • Player Interaction and Endgame: Player interaction is critical, with strategies involving cooperation and competition.

    Players can work together or betray alliances, depending on the situation. The game ends when a single team dominates the petri dish.

    However, with the ability to rejoin other teams, the path to victory is always challenging.

  • Target Audience and Implementation: â€œVoyage of Bacteriaâ€ is designed for board game enthusiasts and students interested in biology.

    With a mix of fun game-play and scientific knowledge, it offers a unique blend of entertainment and education.

    Collaboration with board game companies and hosting game sessions at schools can help spread the word and gather feedback to refine the experience.

    Ultimately, this game teaches synthetic biology in an engaging format and fosters critical thinking, teamwork, and strategic planning. Itâ€™s an exciting way to make science more accessible and enjoyable for all ages.

Education & Communication

This year, we continue the most basic and direct way of science popularization and communication - offline science popularization and project communication between Igem teams. We achieve creativity by exploring different and interesting popular science topics, improvability by getting feedback after class to modify lecture content and collaboration in team communication to improve projects, and engagement through communication in class. A Lecture About Gene at Jianping Experimental School

For primary school students, the knowledge we need to explain to them should not be too difficult, but should be closer to life, so that it is easier for them to understand. So we use movies and cartoons that kids love to extract useful information to teach them about genes.

We use fun rehearsals, visual ICONS and interactive presentations to simplify complex genetic science and make the abstract and mysterious concept of â€œthe blueprint of lifeâ€ - genes - gradually clear in the minds of children.

We talked about the double helix structure of DNA, the transmission of genetic information, and how genes affect our appearance, personality and even disease susceptibility, etc. The children also showed great enthusiasm and repeatedly asked questions, which also made us better understand what parts of children in this age group need more explanation and what knowledge they are more interested in. We also modified our lecture notes to complete the process of two-way learning.

A Lecture About PACIFY at Xiangnan Road Neighborhood Committee

Publicity is necessary in the course of our project. Itch and Staphylococcus aureus are very relevant to our lives, so we choose to publicize in the community, facing people of different ages.

We explained why the human body feels itch, described the morphological characteristics of Staphylococcus aureus, life habits, and how it is under certain conditions, such as skin damage or decreased immunity, to take advantage of the opportunity to cause skin infection, and then lead to itching, redness and other uncomfortable symptoms.

At the same time, we also explained the effective ways to relieve itching and resist bacterial infection to the audience through interaction, which are very useful methods in life, and we think it is very meaningful.

Finally, we also reflect on the class situation. For audiences of different ages, we need to find a teaching method that can be understood by all, and we also need to refine the content of the lecture so that it can be more attractive to the audience.

ZINE

A DIY publication that encourages creative works about synthetic biology

Why we do this

To be more creative

Education needs not only interesting content, but also more creative forms. Zine printing is relatively simple, requiring only one a4 sheet of paper, but allows participants to create their own small book by hand.

We hope that this novel format will encourage more participants to actively create and share their perspectives on synthetic biology.

To be more improvable

The focus of the zine is to encourage thinking and creation, and its content is not time-sensitive and can be used continuously in the future.

Here is a pdf of the Chinese and English versions for other igem groups who may need it. It can be used to take notes and impressions for students attending lectures, or it can be used to hold cartoon competitionsâ€¦

To be more engaging

Traditional forms of education, such as lectures and picture books, are based on input, but we hope to encourage participants to convey their own ideas about synthetic biology through this project, so that they can further improve their understanding and understanding of this discipline through spontaneous thinking.

A unique approach to understanding the public about our project and synthetic biology

A zine refers to a self-published, small, or noncommercial booklet, which is created by an individual. These small publications can take the form of different sizes, formats, and shapes, and cover a variety of topics.

We designed this zine with the theme of synthetic biology and distributed it offline. Participants can fold and cut the paper into a self-decorating booklet, in which they record their knowledge and thinking about synthetic biology.

The contents of the inner pages have also been carefully designed, mainly guided questions, encouraging participants not only to use words, but also to create in various forms such as painting, collage, etc.

People of different ages can create content of different difficulty according to their understanding of the subject.

We hope that in this activity, participants of different ages and knowledge levels can give play to their strengths and get an immersive creative experience. In addition, in future designs, posters or questionnaires can also be added to the back of the zine to help promote or get feedback.
